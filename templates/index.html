I.

Mathematical Background

Given a square system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, the LU decomposition factors $A$ into a lower-triangular matrix $L$ and an upper-triangular matrix $U$ such that $A = LU$. Once this factorization is available, the system can be solved by two triangular substitutions — much cheaper than re-doing Gaussian elimination for each new right-hand side.

The Doolittle convention

LU factorization is not unique. The Doolittle variant fixes the diagonal of $L$ to be $1$. For an $n \times n$ matrix this gives exactly $n^2$ unknowns ($n(n-1)/2$ in $L$ below the diagonal and $n(n+1)/2$ in $U$ on and above), matching the $n^2$ entries of $A$.

Construction formulas

Expanding $A = LU$ entry by entry and exploiting the triangular structure gives, for $k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$:

$$ u_{kj} \;=\; a_{kj} \;-\; \sum_{p=1}^{k-1} \ell_{kp}\, u_{pj}, \qquad j = k, k+1, \ldots, n $$ $$ \ell_{ik} \;=\; \frac{1}{u_{kk}}\!\left(a_{ik} \;-\; \sum_{p=1}^{k-1} \ell_{ip}\, u_{pk}\right), \qquad i = k+1, \ldots, n $$

Each step uses only entries computed in earlier steps, so the decomposition proceeds row-by-row from the top.

Solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with $LU$

Once $A = LU$, the system $LU\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is split:

Forward substitution

Solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$ from top to bottom:

$$ y_i \;=\; b_i \;-\; \sum_{j=1}^{i-1} \ell_{ij}\, y_j $$

(no division — $L$ has 1's on the diagonal.)

Backward substitution

Solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ from bottom to top:

$$ x_i \;=\; \frac{1}{u_{ii}}\!\left(y_i \;-\; \sum_{j=i+1}^{n} u_{ij}\, x_j\right) $$

Existence and partial pivoting

A Doolittle factorization exists when every leading principal minor of $A$ is non-zero. If a zero pivot $u_{kk}=0$ is encountered, the decomposition fails. The classic remedy is partial pivoting, which produces a permuted factorization $PA = LU$, where $P$ is a permutation matrix that records row swaps. The calculator below supports both variants.

Computational cost

The decomposition itself costs $\mathcal{O}(2n^3/3)$ floating-point operations — the same as Gaussian elimination. Each subsequent back/forward substitution costs only $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$, which is the whole point: factor once, solve many times.

II.

Worked Example 1 — a 3×3 system

Problem.

Solve the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ where

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & -2\\ -4 & 6 & 3\\ -4 & -2 & 8 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 9 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}. $$

Using the Doolittle LU decomposition method (no pivoting needed).

Step 1 · Compute row 1 of $U$ ($k=1$)

With no terms in the summation:

$$ u_{11} = a_{11} = 2 \qquad u_{12} = a_{12} = -1 \qquad u_{13} = a_{13} = -2 $$

Step 2 · Compute column 1 of $L$ ($k=1$)

$$ \ell_{21} = \frac{a_{21}}{u_{11}} = \frac{-4}{2} = -2 $$ $$ \ell_{31} = \frac{a_{31}}{u_{11}} = \frac{-4}{2} = -2 $$

Step 3 · Compute row 2 of $U$ ($k=2$)

$$ u_{22} = a_{22} - \ell_{21}u_{12} = 6 - (-2)(-1) = 6 - 2 = 4 $$ $$ u_{23} = a_{23} - \ell_{21}u_{13} = 3 - (-2)(-2) = 3 - 4 = -1 $$

Step 4 · Compute column 2 of $L$ ($k=2$)

$$ \ell_{32} = \frac{a_{32} - \ell_{31}u_{12}}{u_{22}} = \frac{-2 - (-2)(-1)}{4} = \frac{-2-2}{4} = -1 $$

Step 5 · Compute row 3 of $U$ ($k=3$)

$$ u_{33} = a_{33} - \ell_{31}u_{13} - \ell_{32}u_{23} = 8 - (-2)(-2) - (-1)(-1) = 8 - 4 - 1 = 3 $$

Final factorization

$$ L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ -2 & 1 & 0\\ -2 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad U = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & -2\\ 0 & 4 & -1\\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Verification: $LU = A$.

Step 6 · Forward substitution: $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$

$$ y_1 = b_1 = -1 $$ $$ y_2 = b_2 - \ell_{21}y_1 = 9 - (-2)(-1) = 9 - 2 = 7 $$ $$ y_3 = b_3 - \ell_{31}y_1 - \ell_{32}y_2 = -2 - (-2)(-1) - (-1)(7) = -2 - 2 + 7 = 3 $$ $$ \mathbf{y} = (-1,\; 7,\; 3)^{\!\top} $$

Step 7 · Backward substitution: $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$

$$ x_3 = \frac{y_3}{u_{33}} = \frac{3}{3} = 1 $$ $$ x_2 = \frac{y_2 - u_{23}x_3}{u_{22}} = \frac{7 - (-1)(1)}{4} = \frac{8}{4} = 2 $$ $$ x_1 = \frac{y_1 - u_{12}x_2 - u_{13}x_3}{u_{11}} = \frac{-1 - (-1)(2) - (-2)(1)}{2} = \frac{-1 + 2 + 2}{2} = \frac{3}{2} $$

Solution

$$ \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3/2 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
III.

Worked Example 2 — a 4×4 system

Problem.

Solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 3 & -1 & -1 & 2 \\ -1 & 2 & 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}. $$

Step 1 · Row 1 of $U$, then column 1 of $L$ ($k=1$)

$$ u_{11}=1,\; u_{12}=1,\; u_{13}=0,\; u_{14}=3 $$ $$ \ell_{21}=\tfrac{2}{1}=2,\quad \ell_{31}=\tfrac{3}{1}=3,\quad \ell_{41}=\tfrac{-1}{1}=-1 $$

Step 2 · Row 2 of $U$ ($k=2$)

$$ u_{22} = 1 - (2)(1) = -1 $$ $$ u_{23} = -1 - (2)(0) = -1 $$ $$ u_{24} = 1 - (2)(3) = -5 $$

Step 3 · Column 2 of $L$ ($k=2$)

$$ \ell_{32} = \frac{-1 - (3)(1)}{-1} = \frac{-4}{-1} = 4 $$ $$ \ell_{42} = \frac{2 - (-1)(1)}{-1} = \frac{3}{-1} = -3 $$

Step 4 · Row 3 of $U$ ($k=3$)

$$ u_{33} = -1 - (3)(0) - (4)(-1) = -1 + 4 = 3 $$ $$ u_{34} = 2 - (3)(3) - (4)(-5) = 2 - 9 + 20 = 13 $$

Step 5 · Column 3 of $L$ ($k=3$)

$$ \ell_{43} = \frac{3 - (-1)(0) - (-3)(-1)}{3} = \frac{3 - 3}{3} = 0 $$

Step 6 · Row 4 of $U$ ($k=4$)

$$ u_{44} = -1 - (-1)(3) - (-3)(-5) - (0)(13) = -1 + 3 - 15 = -13 $$

Final factorization

$$ L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 2 & 1 & 0 & 0\\ 3 & 4 & 1 & 0\\ -1 & -3 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 & 3\\ 0 & -1 & -1 & -5\\ 0 & 0 & 3 & 13\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -13 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 7 · Forward substitution

$$ y_1 = 4 $$ $$ y_2 = 1 - (2)(4) = -7 $$ $$ y_3 = -3 - (3)(4) - (4)(-7) = -3 - 12 + 28 = 13 $$ $$ y_4 = 4 - (-1)(4) - (-3)(-7) - (0)(13) = 4 + 4 - 21 = -13 $$

Step 8 · Backward substitution

$$ x_4 = \frac{-13}{-13} = 1 $$ $$ x_3 = \frac{13 - (13)(1)}{3} = \frac{0}{3} = 0 $$ $$ x_2 = \frac{-7 - (-1)(0) - (-5)(1)}{-1} = \frac{-7+5}{-1} = 2 $$ $$ x_1 = \frac{4 - (1)(2) - (0)(0) - (3)(1)}{1} = -1 $$

Solution

$$ \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -1\\ 2\\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
